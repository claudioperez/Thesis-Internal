

\section{Tangent and Adjoint Differentiation}

A state \(\bm{x}\) is in equilibrium if the following equation is satisfied:
\begin{equation}
    \left<\Residual (\bm{x},\AutoParameters{}),\; \bm{\eta}\right> = 0
    \qquad\forall \bm{\eta}
\end{equation}
Linearization furnishes:
\begin{equation}
\left<
   \partial_{\bm{x}} \Residual [\dot{\bm{x}}] 
 + \partial_{\AutoParameters{}} \Residual [\dot{\AutoParameters{}}]
 ,\; \bm{\eta}
\right>
+\left<\Residual,\; \dot{\bm{\eta}}\right> = 0
\end{equation}

For a smooth map $\bm{F}:\mathbb{R}^{n_p}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^{n_u}$ and directions
$(\dot{\AutoParameters{}},\dot{\bm{u}})$, define the linearization
$\partial\bm{F}(\AutoParameters{})$.  
Then
%
\[
    \left< \partial\bm{F}(\AutoParameters{})\,\dot{\AutoParameters{}},\,\bm{\eta} \right>
    =
    \left< \dot{\AutoParameters{}},\, \partial\bm{F}(\AutoParameters{})^{\!\mathsf{T}}\bm{\eta} \right>
\]
%
Forward mode computes the left‐hand JVP, reverse mode the right‐hand VJP.  
One chooses the cheaper side of the equality: forward when $n_p$ is small, reverse
when $n_u$ (or the number of scalar objectives) is small.

\subsection{Summary}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Aspect} & \textbf{Forward / Direct} & \textbf{Reverse / Adjoint} \\
\midrule
Linear object & JVP: $\partial\bm{F}\,\dot{\AutoParameters{}}$ &
                 VJP: $\bm{\eta}^{\mathsf{T}}\partial\bm{F}$\\
Cost scaling  & $\mathcal{O}(n_p)$ & $\mathcal{O}(n_g)$ \\
Memory        & constant (streaming) & $\propto$ depth  \\
Best for      & few inputs, many outputs &
                 many inputs, few outputs \\
Classical names &
  Direct/tangent, TLM, DDM &
  Adjoint method, backprop, reverse mode \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}


Reverse mode is attractive when $n_g \ll n_p$, e.g.
\begin{itemize}
  \item gradient‐based optimization of a scalar loss depending on
        millions of design variables,
  \item machine‐learning training where $\AutoParameters{}$ are network weights.
\end{itemize}

Forward mode is attractive when $n_p \ll n_g$, where $n_g$ is the number of
scalar outputs whose derivatives are required.  
Typical examples are
\begin{itemize}
  \item design of a single component subject to many sensor outputs, or
  \item time–integration of ODEs/PDEs where sensitivities to a small set of
        initial conditions are sought at \emph{all} time steps.
\end{itemize}
