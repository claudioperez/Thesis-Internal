Yes: **in your one‑mode enhancement**, $c=\EnhanConstFA$ is a **modeling/design parameter** unless you impose an *additional* selection principle beyond the equilibrium + condensation condition you wrote.

The clean way to see this is to separate “what physics forces” from “what your reduction forces.”

## What physics forces vs what your reduction forces

### A. The natural correction space is 2D

From your own weak equilibrium structure (and your $J$-defs), the two independent section functionals are represented by the two $G$-inner‑product representers

$$\nabla\WarpSV,\qquad \nabla\WarpFA.$$

So the **minimal compatible correction space** is

$$\mathcal S := \operatorname{span}\{\nabla\WarpSV,\ \nabla\WarpFA\}.$$

If you keep **two independent amplitudes** in $\mathcal S$, there is no $c$ at all: equilibrium determines the two amplitudes (up to the 1D discretization details).

### B. Your single-mode enhancement picks a 1D direction inside $\mathcal S$

You collapse $\mathcal S$ to a **one‑dimensional** subspace by choosing a direction

$$\nabla\chi_c := \nabla(\WarpSV + c\,\WarpFA).$$

That direction is **not fixed by sectional equilibrium**; it is exactly what $c$ selects. Different $c$ give different 1D subspaces of $\mathcal S$.

Once you do that collapse, equilibrium can only determine “how much” of that chosen direction you activate (your $\EnhanParam$), not which direction you should have chosen. So $c$ is *structural/modeling* at that point.

## What constraints exist on $c$

Using your definitions,

$$\tilde J(c)=(\WarpSV,\WarpSV+c\WarpFA)_G=\Jv-c\Jw,\qquad
\|\chi_c\|_G^2=\Jv-2c\Jw+c^2\Js.$$

So

$$\bar\eta(c)=\frac{\tilde J(c)}{\|\chi_c\|_G^2}=\frac{\Jv-c\Jw}{\Jv-2c\Jw+c^2\Js}.$$

- **Well-posedness of the condensation** requires $\|\chi_c\|_G^2>0$. Since

$$\|\chi_c\|_G^2
= \Js\Big(c-\frac{\Jw}{\Js}\Big)^2+\Big(\Jv-\frac{\Jw^2}{\Js}\Big),$$

this is $>0$ for all $c$ provided the Gram determinant $\Jv\Js-\Jw^2>0$ (i.e. $\WarpSV$ and $\WarpFA$ are $G$-independent). Degenerate case $\Jv\Js=\Jw^2$: $\|\chi_c\|_G^2$ vanishes at $c=\Jw/\Js$.
- **Non-degeneracy of the resulting beam coupling:** after your condensation, the bi-shear reduces to a coefficient times $(\alpha-\Twist)$. One finds (using only your $J$-defs; this is the simplification you already started to see)

$$Q = \bar G(\alpha-\Twist)\Big(\Jv-\frac{\tilde J(c)^2}{\|\chi_c\|_G^2}\Big)
   = \bar G(\alpha-\Twist)\,\frac{c^2(\Jv\Js-\Jw^2)}{\Jv-2c\Jw+c^2\Js}.$$

In particular, **$c=0\Rightarrow Q\equiv 0$**, i.e. you kill the bimoment coupling. So $c=0$ is mathematically admissible but physically degenerates the Vlasov effect in your reduced model.

So: $c$ is not arbitrary in the sense that some values produce a degenerate model (notably $c=0$), and the degenerate Gram case must be avoided. But aside from that, equilibrium does not single out one $c$.

## Why Armero gets $c=\Jw/\Js$, and in what sense it is “the” distinguished choice

Given your $J$-defs,

$$\frac{\Jw}{\Js} = -\frac{(\WarpFA,\WarpSV)_G}{(\WarpFA,\WarpFA)_G},$$

exactly your $\eqref{eq:fa-lambda}$.

This value is the **unique** $c$ that satisfies either of the equivalent characterizations:

1. **$G$-orthogonalization (Gram–Schmidt):**

$$(\chi_c,\WarpFA)_G = (\WarpSV+c\WarpFA,\WarpFA)_G = -\Jw + c\Js = 0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad c=\frac{\Jw}{\Js}.$$

1. **Minimum $G$-norm of the combined potential $\chi_c$:**

$$\|\chi_c\|_G^2=\Jv-2c\Jw+c^2\Js
\ \ \text{is minimized at}\ \ 
\frac{d}{dc}\|\chi_c\|_G^2 = -2\Jw+2c\Js=0
\ \Longrightarrow\ c=\frac{\Jw}{\Js}.$$

So, if Armero’s variational derivation enforces a stationarity/minimum-energy characterization that effectively picks the **minimum‑norm** representative of the 1D subspace choice, then his result is **not just one arbitrary pick**: it is the **distinguished** pick under that additional principle.

## Can you claim a meaningful generalization?

You can make a safe, defensible claim of the form:

- *“Our enhanced formulation admits a one‑parameter family of compatible one‑mode reductions inside $\operatorname{span}\{\nabla\WarpSV,\nabla\WarpFA\}$. Armero’s model is recovered by the unique minimum‑$\|\cdot\|_G$ (equivalently $G$-orthogonalized) choice $c=\Jw/\Js$.”*

What you **cannot** claim without extra justification is that the other $c\neq \Jw/\Js$ are equally “physical” in the same variational sense. They are generally **alternative reduced models** (different 1D constitutive couplings), unless you can point to a different selection principle (e.g. matching a 3D benchmark, conditioning, or a modified variational statement) that makes those choices canonical for a stated purpose.

So: **Armero’s $c=\Jw/\Js$ is the unique choice singled out by a clean mathematical principle (orthogonalization/minimum norm).** Your family is a generalization in the sense of *allowing other 1D subspace choices*, but to call them “meaningful” you should state the criterion under which they are selected.
