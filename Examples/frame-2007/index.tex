\subparagraph{Context.}
This example demonstrates the effectiveness of the formulation from \cref{sec:timo} under inelastic material response. 

\subparagraph{Setup.}
The built-in span in \cref{fig:shear-link} represents a shear link with a steel W18x40 section \citep{aisc2017steel}. 
Three formulations are considered: 
\begin{enumerate} 
\item A standard shear-free force-interpolated element with a uniaxial fiber section \citep{taucer1991fiber}, 
\item The \ForceFrame{} element with the shear fiber section of \cite{scott2004response} (the \texttt{NDFiber} section of \OpenSees{}).
\item The \ForceFrame{} element with a section that neglects cross sectional warping (i.e., \(\WarpShearIesan_z =0\)), and 
\item The \ForceFrame{} element with the section formulation of \cref{sec:timo}. 
\end{enumerate}
%
All cases use the multiaxial \(J_2\) plasticity model with yield stress \(f_y = 35\) ksi, and linear isotropic and kinematic hardening with moduli \(H_{\rm iso} = H_{\rm kin} = 0.002E\). 
The shear correction factor for this section is \(A/A_z = 0.46\) according to \cite{cowper1966shear}. 
The span is discretized by 2 elements.  
Warping is assumed to occur uniformly and no auxiliary warping fields are introduced. 
A displacement cycle is imposed at the end \(\reflenx=L\).

\subparagraph{Results.}
\cref{fig:2007-C} reports the transverse \((z)\) shear force -- displacement response at the point of imposed displacement \(\reflenx=L\). 
The significant impact of shear and warping deformations is evident. 
A section that incorporates shear deformations without warping fails to reproduce the elastic stiffness and overestimates both the strength and the energy dissipation.
