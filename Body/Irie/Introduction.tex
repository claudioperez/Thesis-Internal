Over the past two decades performance‑based methodologies have reshaped
structural engineering practice by demanding ever more faithful
simulations of how bridges behave under extreme loading. Earthquakes
remain the dominant driver, but blast, vehicular impact, scour, and
progressive collapse scenarios all place similar expectations on
computational models: they must accommodate nonlinear material
behavior; capture large‑displacement kinematics; and execute robust,
dynamically stable solution algorithms. At the same time, the
proliferation of long‑term monitoring networks has created a parallel
demand for data‑centric identification methods that can translate
ambient or event‑recorded vibrations into quantitative measures of
health. High‑fidelity finite‑element analysis and data‑driven system
identification therefore coexist as complementary lenses through which
resilience is assessed.

Delivering these capabilities at operational speed requires software
that is not only numerically efficient but also flexible enough to
absorb new element formulations, identification techniques, and
computing paradigms as they emerge. Previous frameworks have
demonstrated how careful separation of concerns and disciplined use of
composition can yield simulation environments that remain viable across
multiple research generations. Building on that lineage, the present
framework unifies physics‑based prediction and data‑driven inference
behind a common set of abstractions---\textbf{Asset, Event, Predictor,
Metric,} and \textbf{Evaluation}---so that analysts can mix and match
numerical engines without rewriting orchestration logic.

Each abstraction is realized through a modest interface that hides
implementation details while exposing the minimum behavior required for
collaboration. Predictors, for example, are interchangeable whether they
wrap a nonlinear time‑history analysis or a stochastic
subspace‑identification routine; both accept the same event records and
both emit metrics in a standard format understood by the evaluation
layer. 
The resulting architecture balances computational performance
with a level of flexibility that allows it to track advances in material
models, solution algorithms, surrogate learning, and parallel hardware.
