\subsection{Literature}

Capacity limit states are often absent from post-event decision support, particularly for older nonductile bridge columns. 
\citet{zhou2022capacitya} highlight this gap in the context of ShakeCast and develop component capacity limit states for pre-1971 columns, spanning minor damage through collapse. 
\citet{zhou2022capacitya} show that ductility can be a reasonable indicator for minor to moderate damage under cyclic loading, but that it becomes inconsistent for severe damage under earthquake loading as peak ductility can occur before bar buckling and severe strength deterioration. 
Their work motivates an explicit strain-based mapping, which provides a direct link between simulated response and operational guidance.
The report synthesizes an experimental database of nonductile columns to ground the taxonomy in observable damage mechanisms, and documents both the scarcity of data at the most severe states and the sensitivity of ductility-based thresholds under earthquake loading. 
These observations reinforce the need for damage metrics that are physically interpretable and compatible with the strain fields produced by nonlinear simulation.

\section{Limit State Taxonomy}\label{sec:t1-taxonomy}

The damage state mapping for bridge columns is based on fiber strains, which link computed response to observable phenomena such as cover cracking, spalling, and bar buckling. 
In the present framework, columns are modeled with distributed-inelasticity frame elements and fiber-discretized sections. 
For each column, strain histories are computed at representative fibers and reduced to maxima that define the damage state.


For nonductile bridge columns, \citet{zhou2022capacitya} propose a seven-state taxonomy (LS$_0$--LS$_7$) with associated consequences that range from no action and patching at low damage to jacketing, column replacement, and bridge replacement at the highest damage states. 
Their classification connects recognizable damage mechanisms, including cover cracking and spalling, exposure of the confined core, bar buckling, and loss of lateral or vertical capacity, to outcomes that can be operationalized in a post-event workflow. 
This taxonomy provides a useful anchor for the metric mapping employed on the \gls{platform}, which retains the same qualitative progression while expressing each state in terms of computed material strains. 
In their classification, the early states are tied to cracking and spalling of the cover, intermediate states to the onset and progression of crushing into the confined core, and the severe states to bar buckling, possible rupture, and collapse identified by a significant loss of lateral capacity \citep{zhou2022capacitya}. 
The study also notes that data supporting some transition thresholds are sparse, particularly at the highest damage states, since many experiments terminate once lateral strength has degraded by roughly 20\% \citep{zhou2022capacitya}. 
This motivates a cautious interpretation of collapse states and underscores the need for calibration as more observed event data are accumulated.

Let the outer concrete boundary and the core boundary be
\[
\Gamma_o := \partial \Section_{\mathrm c},
\qquad
\Gamma_i := \partial \SectionCore.
\]
Then
\[
\partial \SectionCover = \Gamma_o \cup \Gamma_i,
\]
so \(\partial\SectionCover\) should not be used when only the outermost or innermost cover fibers are intended.

For \(\bm r\in\SectionCover\), define
\[
d_o(\bm r) := \operatorname{dist}(\bm r,\Gamma_o),
\qquad
d_i(\bm r) := \operatorname{dist}(\bm r,\Gamma_i),
\qquad
\delta_{\mathrm{cov}}(\bm r) := \frac{d_o(\bm r)}{d_o(\bm r)+d_i(\bm r)}.
\]
For \(0\le a\le b\le 1\), define the cover band
\[
\SectionCover[a,b]
:=
\{\bm r\in\SectionCover : a\le \delta_{\mathrm{cov}}(\bm r)\le b\}.
\]
Hence
\[
\SectionCover[0,0]=\Gamma_o,
\qquad
\SectionCover[1,1]=\Gamma_i,
\qquad
\SectionCover\!\left[\tfrac12,\tfrac34\right]
\]
is the mid-to-inner cover band.

d_{\mathrm{core}}(\bm r) := \operatorname{dist}(\bm r,\Gamma_i),
\qquad
d_{\mathrm{core}}^{\max} := \max_{\bm r\in\SectionCore} d_{\mathrm{core}}(\bm r),
\qquad
\delta_{\mathrm{core}}(\bm r) := \frac{d_{\mathrm{core}}(\bm r)}{d_{\mathrm{core}}^{\max}}.

\section{Discussion. Implications and Limitations}\label{sec:t1-discussion}
The strain-based mapping provides a direct, physically interpretable link between nonlinear simulation and actionable reporting, but it also exposes several limitations. 
First, the damage state boundaries in \cref{tab:DS} remain sensitive to uncertainty in material properties, detailing, and confinement, and therefore must be calibrated as additional events are captured. 
Second, the mapping is local by construction; it captures member-level damage but does not directly encode system-level redundancy or load redistribution. 
Finally, the reliance on fiber strains means that model form errors in section kinematics can propagate directly into the reported damage state. 
The enhanced section formulations of Part~I mitigate this by improving the underlying strain field, but continued validation against observed event data remains essential as the platform scales.


The damage state metric can be used to further extend the decision-making process for operational actions with standard practices by Caltrans' Structures Maintenance and Investigations group, such as those outlined in \cite{caltrans2017first}. 
For example, DS1-2 corresponds to allowing the structure to remain open; DS3-4 corresponds to inspecting the structure while allowing it to remain open; DS5 corresponds to closing the bridge for further inspection, and DS6 corresponds to closing the bridge immediately. 
The {\em{decision boundaries}} are to be calibrated in the future as more events occur and are analyzed by the \gls{platform}.
